# Parent Advice Article-Making Rules

## Core principle

Every article must respect the parent’s time.

The standard is:

**Value per word.**

Do not write to reach a word count.
Do not add paragraphs because “articles should be long.”
Do not pad for SEO.
Do not include generic advice that any tutoring website could say.

An article should be only as long as needed to give the parent clarity, confidence, and a sensible next step.

## What a good article should do

A Jothi Parent Advice article should help a parent think more clearly.

It should usually do at least one of these:

* explain a common problem calmly
* correct a misunderstanding
* help the parent notice early warning signs
* give a practical next step
* reduce panic
* show how Jothi thinks about learning, confidence, progress, or exam preparation

The parent should finish thinking:

**“I understand the situation better now.”**

## Target length

Use word count as a guide, not a rule.

* **500–700 words**: good for simple questions
* **700–900 words**: normal target for most Parent Advice articles
* **900–1,100 words**: acceptable only when the topic genuinely needs it
* **Over 1,100 words**: needs a strong reason, or should become two articles or a separate resource

A short article is fine if it is useful.
A longer article is fine if every section earns its place.

## Skimmability standard

Each article should be useful in two ways:

1. **Skim read:** a busy parent should get value in under 1 minute.
2. **Full read:** the article should normally take around 2.5–4 minutes.

Use:

* clear headings
* short paragraphs
* useful bullet lists
* occasional bold skim lines
* practical examples where they add clarity

Do not over-format. The article should feel calm and readable, not busy.

## Content quality rule

Before keeping a section, ask:

* Does this move the parent forward?
* Does this reduce confusion?
* Does this give a useful next step?
* Does this show Jothi judgement?
* Would a busy parent feel respected after reading this?

If the answer is no, cut it.

## Jothi voice

The tone should be:

* calm
* practical
* thoughtful
* parent-facing
* warm but not sentimental
* clear but not simplistic
* expert without sounding academic

Avoid:

* hype
* fear-based selling
* generic SEO filler
* long theoretical explanations
* internal Jothi language
* unnecessary education jargon

## Use Jothi judgement, not generic advice

The article should include at least one useful Jothi-style insight where appropriate.

Examples:

* confidence often drops before marks drop
* marks are symptoms, not the full diagnosis
* summer is for taking stock, not panic
* exam board → subject → topic is a better order than vague revision
* momentum often comes before motivation
* rapport comes before remedy
* the aim is to identify the right next step, not overload the child

These ideas should be explained in parent language.

## Resource links and heavy detail

Do not overload advice articles with long link lists, specification links, tables, or resource directories.

If a resource is valuable but heavy, create a separate resource page later.

The article should explain the thinking.
The resource page can hold the links, checklists, or detailed tools.

## Final article test

Before approving an article, ask:

**Would I be happy for a thoughtful parent to read this before booking a consultation?**

Then ask:

**Did we respect their time?**

If yes, the article is good enough for the draft library.
